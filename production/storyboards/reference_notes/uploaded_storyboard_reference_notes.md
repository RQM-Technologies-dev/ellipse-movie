# Uploaded Storyboard Reference Notes

These notes summarize the storyboard reference frames uploaded during planning. They define the desired storyboard-generation direction for Ellipse boards going forward.

## Reference set reviewed

- `1A` / `1Aa` — wide landscape staging with Aaron as a small figure against Tara-scale geography.
- `3A` / `4A` — Elgia and port arrival boards with ship rigging, harbor geography, and scale relationships.
- `5A` — Aaron and Aura riding through an urban street, character staging integrated into environment.
- `6A` — Teacher / library board with strong interior architecture, shelves, balconies, and character power dynamics.
- `7A` — close object board, hand and glowing gem as a clear visual story beat.
- `8A` — embrace inside swirling energy, character anchor retained inside spectacle.
- `9A` — Kingdom arrival, characters small against vast palace/cosmic geography.
- `10A` — Portara chamber, gate scale, character positions, and final-threshold blocking.

## What the references establish

### 1. Storyboards should look like production boards, not concept art

The references are black-and-white hand-drawn storyboard panels with visible pencil construction, confident sketch lines, and a clear frame border. They are detailed enough for blocking and camera planning, but they are not polished illustrations or final concept art.

### 2. Environmental detail must be higher

The example boards include a lot of environment information: mountains, foliage, ship rigging, harbor buildings, bookshelves, balconies, stair/door architecture, palace silhouettes, and crystal structures. Future prompts should ask for this level of environmental specificity.

### 3. Characters are scale anchors

The figures are often placed inside a larger environment rather than filling the whole frame. This is especially important for Tara, Eros, the Academy, the Kingdom, the Garden, and the Portara.

### 4. Foreground / midground / background should be explicit

The best references create depth with foreground framing elements, clear midground character action, and detailed background geography. Prompts should explicitly request layered staging.

### 5. Boards should preserve cinematic motion

These are not disconnected drawings. They imply camera movement and story handoff: a character looks, the camera reveals; a horse exits; a figure approaches a doorway; a hand reveals the gem; an embrace anchors cosmic motion.

### 6. Spectacle must keep a human anchor

The gem, cosmic embrace, palace, and Portara boards work because the fantasy element is anchored by a hand, body, silhouette, or character relationship. Prompts should not produce abstract spectacle without staging.

## Prompt-generation implications

Every future storyboard prompt should include:

- black-and-white pencil storyboard style,
- wide cinematic panel with clean border,
- optional blank header/slate area, but no generated text labels,
- high environment detail,
- clear foreground/midground/background staging,
- readable geography,
- character scale and posture,
- visual handoff to the next board,
- rough production-board linework rather than polished art.

## Things to avoid

- overly polished digital concept art,
- photorealism,
- color render unless specifically requested,
- empty backgrounds,
- generic fantasy illustration,
- abstract action without readable geography,
- character portraits that ignore blocking,
- text labels generated inside the image.
